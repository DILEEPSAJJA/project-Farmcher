Jalapeño (Capsicum annuum) is a medium-sized chili pepper that is commonly used in Mexican cuisine. It is a member of the nightshade family, which includes other peppers, tomatoes, and potatoes. Jalapeños are typically harvested when they are green and are approximately 2-3 inches (5-8 cm) long, but they can also be left to ripen to red or brown.

These chilies are medium to hot in spiciness and are commonly used in Mexican and Tex-Mex cuisine

Jalapeño peppers grow on a bushy plant that can reach a height of up to 3 feet (1 meter) and a width of 2-3 feet (0.6-0.9 meters). The plant has dark green, glossy leaves and produces small, white flowers that give way to the peppers.

Jalapeño peppers can be grown from seeds or seedlings. They prefer well-draining soil that is rich in organic matter and a warm, sunny location. The seeds can be started indoors in late winter or early spring and transplanted to the garden after the last frost date. Alternatively, they can be sown directly into the garden after the soil has warmed up.

The plants should be spaced about 18-24 inches (45-60 cm) apart to allow for good air circulation and to prevent the spread of diseases. Jalapeño plants require regular watering, especially during hot, dry weather, and should be fertilized regularly with a balanced fertilizer.

Jalapeño peppers will typically begin to produce fruit 70-80 days after planting, and the fruit can be harvested when they are green and firm. They can also be left to ripen to red or brown, although the heat may increase as the peppers ripen.
